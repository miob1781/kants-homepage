Teaching

I have been teaching for more than forty years. My lectures include classes on philosophical disciplines along with ones addressed at a broader audience. Click on the titles of the lectures below for more information.

Anthropology

This lecture is aimed at a broader audience. I discuss mankind from a pragmatic point of view. Beginning with the anthropological didactic, I introduce the faculty of cognition (including outer and inner sense, imagination, and understanding), the feeling of pleasure or displeasure, and the faculty of desire. Afterwards, the anthropological characteristic takes a look at the character of person, sex, people, race, and human species. The aim of this lecture is to make listeners, irrespective of faculty, familiar with human natur.

Physical Geography

This lecture is aimed at a broader audience. While my anthropology lecture introduces human nature, this lecture is concerned with the Earth. In the first part, I present aspects of the water, the land, the atmosphere, and the natural history of our planet. Then the second part discusses the creatures inhabiting the Earth. Finally, the third part deals with the contintents.

Logic

The lecture on logic teaches the nature of cognition. I begin by introducing the notion of cognition and discussing various aspects of it. Afterwards, the elementary doctrine deals with concepts, judgments, and inferences. I conclude with some remarks about methodology.

Textbook: G. F. Meier, Auszug aus der Vernunftlehre, 1752.

Metaphysics

Metaphysics is the science of things as such and divides into general metaphyics (ontology) and special metaphysics. Ontology takes the faculty of the understanding as a guiding thread and develops the table of categories from it, which are the fundamental metaphysical concepts. Special metaphysics divides into rational psychology, cosmology, and rational theology.

Textbook: A.G. Baumgarten, Metaphysica, 1757.

Moral Philosophy

My lecture on moral philosophy concerns the system of ethical duties, beginnning with the supreme moral principle: the categorical imperative. While I shall talk about particular duties, I also address fundamental practical concepts like necessitation and law.

Textbook: A.G. Baumgarten, Initia philosophiae practicae primae, 1760.

Rational Theology

Rational Theology is the cognition of God from reason. While I develop the concept of God in great detail, I will refute all possible proofs of his existence. Nonetheless, the concept of God leads to various branches of rational theology, including ontotheology, cosmotheology, physicotheology, and moraltheology.
